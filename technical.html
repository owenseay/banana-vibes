Vibe 01Drift

  • driftX: 10–50px (horizontal float range)
  • driftY: 10–50px (vertical float range)
  • offsetX, offsetY: 0–2π (random phase offset per axis, determines path shape)
  • rotationSpeed: 0.005–0.035
  • rotationAmplitude: 5–50° (scaled to 0.2× for gentle rocking)
  • Motion: sinusoidal on both axes with secondary slower sine layer (÷3 frequency, ±5px)

Why do some of the bananas appear to float back and forth while others move in circles?

Every banana's position is driven by a sine wave on each axis. Think of it like a marble on a dinner plate being rocked by two people: one rocking left-right, one rocking forward-back. driftX is how far the plate tilts horizontally (10 to 50 pixels), driftY is how far it tilts vertically. If one value is much larger than the other, the path is mostly a line along that axis. If they're close to equal, it opens up into an ellipse.

But the shape of the path (line vs circle vs oval) is really determined by the gap between offsetX and offsetY, which are random phase offsets for each axis. If both rockers start at the same moment (gap of 0), the marble slides diagonally. If one is a quarter-cycle ahead of the other (gap of π/2), the marble traces a circle. Everything in between gives you some degree of oval. The individual offset values don't matter at all. Only the gap between them matters.

On top of the primary motion, there's a secondary sine layer running at one third the frequency with ±5px of drift. It's like the table the plate is sitting on is also slowly swaying. This keeps the paths from being perfectly clean repeating loops.

How does the sine function create smooth motion?

Picture a clock with a hand that's 1 unit long. As the hand sweeps around, the tip traces a circle. Now ignore the circle and just watch how high the tip is at any moment. At 3 o'clock the height is 0. At 12 o'clock it's 1. At 9 o'clock it's back to 0. At 6 o'clock it's -1. That height value at any point in the rotation is what sin() returns. It smoothly cycles between -1 and 1, forever.

The code feeds in a time value that keeps ticking up, and sine cycles its output between -1 and 1. Multiply that by driftX (say, 30) and the banana smoothly moves between -30 and +30 pixels from home. Both axes share the same clock. The offsets just shift where each axis is in the cycle at any given moment, which is what creates the different path shapes.

The motion naturally slows down at the edges because of the geometry of the circle. When the clock hand is near the top or bottom, it's moving mostly horizontally, so the height barely changes. When it's at 3 or 9 o'clock, it's moving mostly vertically, so the height changes fast. The banana isn't programmed to decelerate at the edges of its path. It's just what happens when you read the height off a spinning point.

Position Formula

x = baseX + sin(time + offsetX) × driftX + cos(time / 3) × 5

y = baseY + sin(time + offsetY) × driftY + sin(time / 3) × 5

Vibe 02Space

  • heading: random initial direction (0–2π radians)
  • spaceSpeed: 0.15–0.50 px/frame
  • turnRate: ±0.1–0.5 (heading rotation intensity)
  • turnFreq: 0.05–0.15 Hz (how fast heading drifts)
  • spinRate: ±0.05–0.25°/frame (visual tumble)
  • Topology: toroidal (edges wrap to opposite side)
  • Anchors: none

Heading Formula

heading += sin(t × turnFreq + turnPhase) × turnRate × 0.016

x += cos(heading) × spaceSpeed
y += sin(heading) × spaceSpeed

Why do they never come back to where they started?

Every other vibe anchors its bananas to a home position. Drift has baseX/baseY, Breeze has stemX/stemY, Bounce has the floor. The position formula always pulls back toward that anchor. Space doesn't have one. There's no base position in the math at all. Each frame, the banana just moves forward along its current heading. The new position is the old position plus a velocity vector. Nothing references where the banana was born. Nothing pulls it back.

It's the only vibe where a banana's starting position is completely irrelevant after frame one.

What makes the paths curve instead of going in straight lines?

The heading angle isn't fixed. Every frame it gets nudged by the same sine function from Drift, but applied to the direction of travel instead of position: sin(t × turnFreq + turnPhase) × turnRate. So the banana's heading is always slowly rotating. When the sine output is positive, the heading drifts one way. When it crosses zero and goes negative, the heading starts drifting the other way. The result is long, lazy, sweeping arcs that periodically reverse curvature.

Because turnFreq, turnRate, and turnPhase are all randomized per banana, every banana curves at a different rate, in a different direction, on a different cycle. Two bananas might be heading the same way for a while, then one veers off and the other keeps going. The paths never repeat because the interaction between the heading sine wave and the forward motion creates curves that constantly evolve.

What happens when a banana reaches the edge of the screen?

It wraps. If a banana drifts off the right side, it reappears on the left. Off the bottom, back in at the top. The canvas is topologically a torus: the left edge is connected to the right edge, the top is connected to the bottom. It's the same geometry as Pac-Man's map, or the surface of a donut if you unrolled it flat.

No banana is ever destroyed or created. The same 247 wander forever on an infinite looping surface. They just happen to be visible during the portion of their journey that crosses your screen.

Vibe 03Spin

  • spinStyle: 20% constant, 80% dynamic
  • spinRate: 5–15 (constant spinners only)
  • spinBoost: 0 → spikes to 1000–2000, decays at 0.96×/frame
  • Boost trigger: 0.5% chance per frame (only if current boost < 1000)
  • Dynamic base spin: 360° × (1 + 0.5 × sin(time/300 + phase))

Why do some bananas spin at a steady rate while others speed up out of nowhere?

Spin has two types of bananas: constant spinners (20% of them) and dynamic spinners (80%).

Constant spinners just rotate at a fixed rate forever. They each get a random spinRate between 5 and 15, and that's it. They spin at that speed indefinitely. No variation, no surprises.

Dynamic spinners are less predictable. They have a base rotation that oscillates with a sine wave, so their speed is always gently rising and falling. But on top of that, there's a random boost system. Every frame, there's a 0.5% chance a boost fires. When it does, the banana's spinBoost spikes to somewhere between 1000 and 2000, and then decays at 0.96× per frame (same exponential decay as the wind gusts in Breeze, actually). So the banana is cruising along at its normal oscillating speed, and then suddenly rips into a fast spin that gradually winds back down.

The boost can only trigger if the current boost is below 1000, so a banana can't stack boosts on top of each other. It has to mostly wind down before it can fire again so it doesn't get pureed.

Vibe 04Jitter

  • rotationSpeed: 0.005–0.035 (scaled to 10× in update)
  • rotationAmplitude: 5–50° (scaled to 0.5×)
  • Noise injection: (Math.random() - 0.5) × 10 per frame (±5° random)
  • Base oscillation: sinusoidal at t × 0.003 × rotationSpeed × 10 + phase
  • Result: smooth sine + raw per-frame randomness = stochastic tremors

What's actually causing the jitter?

Each banana has a smooth sinusoidal base oscillation, just like Drift. On its own, it would rock back and forth gracefully. But every single frame, a completely random number between -5 and +5 degrees gets generated and slammed directly onto the rotation angle. Fresh randomness, every frame, with no memory of the previous one.

So the banana is trying to follow a smooth path but keeps getting blindsided by random impulses it can't predict. The result is stochastic tremors: small, rapid, randomly determined movements layered on top of an otherwise clean signal. The noise is the cause, the tremors are the visible meltdown.

Unlike Breeze, where the randomness gets filtered through springs and dampers and gently smoothed out over time, Jitter's noise is completely raw. No smoothing, no decay, no physics. Just pure unfiltered chaos dumped straight onto the rotation. That's why they're so frustrated.

Vibe 05Stretch

  • scaleX: 1 + 0.8 × sin(time/600 + phase)
  • scaleY: 1 + 0.8 × cos(time/600 + phase)
  • Scale range: 0.2× to 1.8× on each axis
  • phase: randomized per banana (prevents synchronization)
  • Sin/cos offset creates 90° phase difference between axes (squish/bulge effect)

How does the squish/bulge effect work?

This is a two-line function. Each banana gets scaled on two axes every frame: scaleX uses sine, scaleY uses cosine, both running at the same speed with the same phase. The 0.8 multiplier means each axis ranges from 0.2× (nearly flat) to 1.8× (nearly double size).

The entire trick is that sine and cosine are naturally 90° out of phase. When scaleX is at its peak (wide), scaleY is at its default (normal height). When scaleY hits its peak (tall), scaleX is back to normal. They take turns. So the banana is always being stretched on one axis while the other relaxes. It breathes in and out, endlessly, like something that probably shouldn't be alive.

Each banana's phase is randomized so they don't all pulse together. That's the only source of variation. Same speed, same amplitude, just different starting points in the cycle.

Why does it look like they're moving in and out of the screen?

There's no z-axis. It's purely 2D scaling. But when scaleX shrinks while scaleY grows, the banana gets narrow and tall, which your brain reads as "turning away from me." When scaleX grows while scaleY shrinks, it gets wide and flat, which reads as "facing me." The continuous cycling between those states creates the illusion that the banana is slowly rotating in 3D space.

The code isn't simulating 3D at all. It's just alternating which axis gets stretched, and your brain fills in the rest.

Vibe 06Breeze

Spring-Damper System

  • kBase: 0.016–0.036 (spring stiffness)
  • dBase: 0.08–0.18 (damping coefficient)
  • response: 0.02–0.05 (target tracking speed)
  • maxAngle: 28–40°
  • restAngle: ±3° initial (drifts slowly over time)
  • restDrift: decays at 0.985×, perturbed by ±0.006/frame
  • Anchor: stemX, stemY (invisible root point)
  • length: size × 0.6 (pendulum arm)

Wind (shared/global)

  • Base wind: 0.18 × sin(t × 0.18) + 0.07 × sin(t × 0.47 + 1.3)
  • Gust trigger: 0.2% chance per frame
  • Gust strength: 0.6–1.3
  • Gust decay: 0.96×/frame
  • Wind tracking: windStrength = windStrength × 0.96 + target × 0.04
  • Wind clamp: ±1.2
  • sensitivity: 0.7–1.6 (per-banana wind response multiplier)
  • Wind → lean: windStrength × 8 × sensitivity

Turbulence (noise lanes)

  • Noise lanes: 2 channels, randomly assigned per banana
  • nRate1: 0.25–0.70 (slow noise frequency)
  • nRate2: 0.60–1.50 (fast noise frequency)
  • nPhase1, nPhase2: randomized per banana
  • Noise smoothing: noise = noise × 0.92 + signal × 0.08
  • airinessDeg: 6–16 (per-banana turbulence response)
  • Turbulence formula: (noise1 + 0.7 × noise2) × airinessDeg

Flutter

  • flutterAmt: 0.12–1.32
  • Formula: sin(t × 6.0 + stemX × 0.01) × flutterAmt × 0.3
  • High-frequency shimmer, position-dependent

Micro-perturbations

  • Random angular velocity spike: 0.2% chance/frame, ±0.06 × (1 + |windStrength|)
  • Constant micro-noise: ±0.0006/frame on angular velocity

Target Angle Composition

rawTarget = baseLean + restAngle + turbulence + flutter → filtered through response rate → fed into spring-damper: angularVel += (kBase × diff - dBase × angularVel) → angle clamped to ±maxAngle (velocity × 0.7 on clamp hit) → position derived: x = stemX + sin(angle) × length, y = stemY + cos(angle) × length

What are decaying wind gusts?

Every frame, there's a 0.2% chance that a random gust fires. When it does, it hits with a strength between 0.6 and 1.3, and then immediately starts fading. Each frame after that, the gust multiplies by 0.96, so it loses 4% of its force every tick. Within a second or two it's basically gone.

That's the "decaying" part. The gust doesn't just switch on and off. It spikes and then bleeds out smoothly, which is what makes the tree feel like it's reacting to real wind rather than being pushed by a metronome. The timing is random, the strength is random, and the falloff is exponential. So you get these unpredictable surges that ripple through the whole tree and then quietly dissolve.

What is spring-damper physics?

Each banana is connected to an invisible anchor point (its "stem") by a virtual spring. The spring pulls the banana back toward its resting position when it swings away, and a damper (like a shock absorber) prevents it from bouncing forever.

So the banana swings out, gets pulled back, overshoots a little, swings out the other way, gets pulled back again, gradually settling. It's the same math that governs a screen door closing or a car's suspension.

In the code, that's the stemX/stemY anchor, a spring stiffness value (kBase), and a damping coefficient (dBase) that bleeds off energy each frame.

What are the noise lanes?

Each banana gets assigned a noiseLane that is either 0 or 1. It's basically splitting the bananas into two groups that respond to different random forces. Think of it like two invisible currents of air flowing through the tree at slightly different patterns. Bananas on lane 0 get nudged by one stream of randomness, bananas on lane 1 get nudged by a different stream. So even though all the bananas are hanging from the same kind of spring and getting hit by the same wind gusts, they don't all move in sync. There's natural variation because half of them are "feeling" slightly different air.

Without the two lanes, every banana would sway in near-unison, which would look mechanical and fake. With them, you get clusters of bananas moving similarly but not identically, which is closer to how real leaves on a tree behave. Groups moving together-ish, but never perfectly synchronized.

"2 Noise Lanes" is technically just saying "we split them into two groups."

But there are multiple layers of bananas. How can they be separated into only 2 lanes?

Yeah, "lanes" is a misleading word because it sounds spatial, like physical rows. They're really just two channels. Two frequencies.

Every banana, regardless of which layer it's on or where it sits in the tree, gets randomly assigned to channel 0 or channel 1 at spawn time. A banana on the front layer and a banana on the back layer might both be on channel 0. Two bananas sitting right next to each other on the same layer might be on different channels. It has nothing to do with position. It's purely a random coin flip that determines which stream of turbulence noise that banana listens to.

But then two bananas on the same channel would move identically?

They would, except each banana also has its own airinessDeg value (randomized between 6 and 16) which acts like a mass coefficient. It's how much that banana cares about the turbulence it's receiving.

A banana with an airinessDeg of 16 is light and fluttery and reacts dramatically to every little gust on its channel. A banana with an airinessDeg of 6 is heavier, more stubborn. It gets the same signal but barely moves.

So even two bananas tuned to the exact same channel will respond to it differently. The channel provides the shared rhythm, the airiness gives each banana its own personality.

What's the difference between the airiness coefficient and the sensitivity value?

They're two separate response multipliers for two separate forces. sensitivity (0.7–1.6) controls how much a banana reacts to the shared global wind (the big, slow, blanket gusts that sway the entire tree). airinessDeg (6–16) controls how much it reacts to the local turbulence coming through its noise lane (the smaller, faster, more chaotic stuff).

So a banana could be very sensitive to the global wind but not very airy. It leans hard with the big gusts but stays relatively still during the turbulence. Or the opposite: barely reacts to the wind but flutters like crazy on its noise channel. They're independent knobs. One governs the macro sway, the other governs the micro behavior.

So there's a shared global wind that applies to the entire tree and then the two noise lanes are nested inside that?

Exactly. The wind is the outermost layer, a slow sweeping force that hits every banana in the tree. It's built from a couple of overlapping sine waves plus random gusts that spike and decay. That's the shared blanket. Every banana feels it, scaled by its own sensitivity value.

Nested inside that, each banana is also listening to its assigned noise lane (channel 0 or channel 1) which adds local turbulence on top of the global wind. And then nested inside that, there's flutter, a tiny high-frequency shimmer that depends on the banana's position in the tree. So it's layers: wind moves the whole tree, turbulence moves groups within the tree, and flutter gives each banana its own little tremor on top of everything else.

Vibe 07Bounce

  • gravity: 0.5–0.55
  • popDelay: 0–5000ms (staggered entry)
  • bounceEnergy: starts at 5–15 (heroes: 18–28)
  • maxBounceEnergy: 15–25
  • Energy gain per bounce: +0.3–0.6 (heroes: +1.2–1.7)
  • Wall collision: velocity × -0.5 (heroes: -0.9)
  • maxVX: regular 6–9, heroes 12–16

Hero Jumpers (8% of bananas)

  • heroCooldown: 2000–5000ms
  • Launch chance: 3% per frame (when cooldown elapsed)
  • Super-arc chance: 20% of hero launches
  • Super-arc energy: 18–22, velocity: ±24
  • Regular hero launch: energy 12–15, velocity: ±14

Trick Flips

  • Trigger: on bounce (heroes 70%, regular 30%)
  • Trick duration: 20–50 frames (heroes: 40–70)
  • Trick rotation speed: heroes 6°/frame, regular 4°/frame
  • Idle rotation: heroes 1.2°/frame, regular 0.5°/frame

How does the bounce physics work?

Bounce is the only vibe that simulates real physics. Every other vibe is purely mathematical: sine waves, scaling, heading rotation. Bounce has gravity, velocity, and collision. Every banana sits on the ground until its popDelay timer expires (staggered 0–5 seconds so they don't all launch at once). Once it pops, gravity takes over. Each frame, gravity gets added to the banana's vertical velocity, pulling it down. When it hits the floor, its vertical velocity gets reversed and set to its current bounceEnergy value, launching it back up.

The key detail is that bounceEnergy increases with every bounce. Each landing adds a small amount of energy (+0.3–0.6 for regular bananas, +1.2–1.7 for heroes), so the bananas gradually bounce higher and higher until they cap out at maxBounceEnergy. They're not losing energy on each bounce like a real ball would. They're gaining it. Every landing is fuel for the next jump.

Wall collisions just reverse horizontal velocity with a multiplier. Regular bananas lose half their speed on impact (×-0.5), so they tend to stay in their lane. Heroes barely lose anything (×-0.9), so they ricochet around the full width of the screen.

What are hero jumpers and trick flips?

8% of bananas are randomly designated as heroes at spawn. They start with more energy, gain energy faster, bounce higher, move faster horizontally, and hit walls harder. They're the ones you notice ripping across the screen while the rest are doing modest little hops.

On top of that, heroes have a launch system. Once their heroCooldown (2–5 seconds) has elapsed, they get a 3% chance per frame of triggering a big launch. 20% of those launches are super-arcs, which spike the energy to 18–22 and fling the banana sideways at ±24 velocity. The banana basically becomes a projectile for a few seconds.

Trick flips trigger on landing. Heroes have a 70% chance of doing one, regular bananas 30%. When a trick fires, the banana's rotation speed jumps from its idle rate (0.5–1.2°/frame) up to 4–6°/frame for a burst of 20–70 frames, then drops back to idle. It's a little mid-air flourish. Heroes spin faster and longer.

This is bananas.
